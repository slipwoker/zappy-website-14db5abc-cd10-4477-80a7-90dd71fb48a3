About SJohnson

I've been building games as an indie game developer because I believe the most memorable experiences come from personal vision and creative risk. Every project is a chance to explore new mechanics, tell stories that matter, and craft worlds that feel genuinely different. I'm not chasing trends—I'm chasing the kind of gameplay that sticks with you long after the credits roll.

Players can expect thoughtful design, attention to atmosphere, and games that respect their time and intelligence. Whether it's a narrative-driven puzzle or an experimental sandbox, my goal is always the same: create something that feels handcrafted, intentional, and uniquely mine.

Now: Currently prototyping a narrative exploration game with branching dialogue systems and learning advanced shader techniques for stylized environmental effects.

Principles

If it isn't fun in grey-box, I don't ship it.

Mechanics that feel alive

Every system should respond with personality. When a player interacts with the world, it should feel like the game is listening and reacting, not just executing code. Physics, AI, and feedback loops are opportunities for character, not just functionality.

Clarity over complexity

Depth doesn't require confusion. The best games have simple rules that create emergent complexity. Players should understand what they can do within seconds, then spend hours discovering what's possible. Elegant design beats feature bloat every time.

Mood and meaning in small details

The way a door opens, the sound of footsteps on different surfaces, the color shift at dusk—these aren't polish, they're the soul of the experience. Players may not consciously notice every detail, but they feel the cumulative weight of care and intention.

How a game becomes real

Prototype fast

Start with the core mechanic—no polish, no perfect art, just the thing that makes the game feel good. Build it in a weekend if you can. The faster you get something playable, the faster you know if it's worth pursuing. Most ideas die here, and that's okay. The ones that survive this phase have something real.

Playtest honestly

Put it in front of people who aren't afraid to tell you it's boring. Watch them play without explaining anything. Their confusion is your roadmap. Their moments of delight are your North Star. Every playtest session rewrites the design doc. Listen to what players do, not just what they say.

Polish the feel

This is where good games become great. Juice the animations, tune the sound effects, add screen shake and particle effects. Make every button press feel satisfying. The difference between a prototype and a finished game is in these tiny details that players might not consciously notice but absolutely feel. This phase takes longer than you think.

Ship and iterate

Launch isn't the finish line—it's the starting gun. Real players will find bugs you never imagined and use your systems in ways you never intended. Update regularly, fix what's broken, and listen to your community. The game you ship is version 1.0. The game it becomes after six months of player feedback is the one people remember.
